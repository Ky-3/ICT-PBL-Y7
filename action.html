Ways to Stop Water Pollution

1. Properly Dispose of Chemicals

Common household items like old paint, motor oil, and harsh cleaning agents contain toxic substances that water treatment plants cannot always filter out. Instead of pouring them down the sink or into street drains, which lead directly to local rivers, store them in a secure container. Check for hazardous waste collection centers or designated local drop-off days to ensure they are neutralized properly.

2. Reduce Plastic Use

Bags and Bottles: Use cloth or jute bags and stainless steel or glass water bottles.
Utensils: Opt for bamboo or steel straws and silverware instead of plastic ones.
Produce: Buy loose fruits and vegetables rather than those pre-wrapped in plastic.

3. Use Eco-Friendly Household Products

Many standard detergents and soaps contain phosphates that cause "dead zones" in lakes by fueling massive algae blooms. Switching to biodegradable, non-toxic, and phosphate-free products ensures that the water leaving your home via the drain is less harmful to the environment.

4. Conserve Water

When you use less water, you reduce the overall volume of wastewater that must be processed by treatment plants, making them more efficient and less likely to overflow during heavy rain. Simple habits like taking shorter showers, turning off the tap while brushing your teeth, and only running the dishwasher when full can save hundreds of gallons per week.

5. Pick Up Pet Waste

Pet waste is more than just an annoyance, it is a major carrier of harmful bacteria and parasites. When it rains, these pathogens wash into storm drains and end up in local swimming and fishing areas. Always bag pet waste and dispose of it in the trash or a toilet to keep these bacteria out of the watershed.

6. Eat More Organic Food

Organic foods tend to be produced with few synthetic chemicals. As a result, eating organic reduces the amount of chemical pollution that ends up in the water. The food we choose to eat can have a big impact on environmental quality — consider the chemicals used to grow food and the fuel used to transport crops and power farm equipment on industrial farms.

7. Don't Use Garden Chemicals

Excess fertilizers and pesticides from lawns are a primary source of runoff pollution. Instead of chemical treatments, try:

Composting: Use organic waste from your kitchen and yard to create nutrient-rich soil naturally.
Mulching Mowers: Leave grass clippings on the lawn to act as a natural fertilizer.
Hardy Plants: Choose local plant varieties that naturally resist pests and require less water and chemical intervention.

8. Participate in Local Cleanups

Join or organize community events to clear trash from beaches, riverbanks, and parks. Removing litter before it enters the water prevents it from becoming a permanent pollutant or hazard to wildlife.

9. Fix Leaky Vehicles

Oil and antifreeze leaking from cars are washed off the road by rain and carried into the nearest body of water. Since one quart of oil can contaminate up to a million gallons of water, regularly checking for leaks and using absorbent materials like kitty litter to clean up spills on your driveway is crucial.

10. Plant Trees and Native Gardens

Plants act as a natural filter for our planet. Trees and shrubs help prevent soil erosion, which otherwise clouds water and smothers aquatic habitats. Planting native species with deep roots also helps rain soak into the ground, filtering out pollutants before the water reaches the underground aquifer.

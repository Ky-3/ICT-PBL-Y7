The pollution described above is not just a scientific issue. It directly impacts our city's development and the daily lives of our residents. In 2026, we are observing several critical risks:

Public Health Risks

Rising rates of waterborne diseases in densely populated wards place an enormous strain on our healthcare facilities.

Economic Degradation

The degradation of the Hlaing River impacts local livelihoods, particularly for those in the fishing and small-scale agricultural sectors.

Loss of Biodiversity

Local urban lakes that once served as natural cooling systems for our neighborhoods are becoming stagnant zones unable to support aquatic life.

1. Microbial Contamination

This is perhaps our most urgent problem. Because our city is growing so fast, the pipes for our sewage system cannot keep up. A lot of household waste just goes straight into the small canals and the big Hlaing River without being cleaned first. This water is full of germs, specifically dangerous bacteria like E. coli. When this gets into the ground where we get our drinking water, it makes people very sick with stomach illnesses.

2. Industrial Effluent

Many factories operate near our water sources, and some do not have the right machines to clean their waste. They pour out "effluent," which is a fancy word for liquid waste from a factory. This liquid is often filled with heavy metals and harsh chemicals that are not supposed to be in nature. These toxins do not just disappear; they sink to the bottom of the river and stay there, poisoning the fish and plants for a long time.

3. Solid Waste Leachate

We see trash everywhere, and it is a major issue for our water. When plastic bottles and other rubbish are dumped on the ground, they don't just sit there. When it rains, water soaks through this pile of trash and picks up all the poisonous chemicals from the rotting waste. This "leachate" acts like a toxic soup that soaks deep into the soil. It eventually reaches our underground water supplies, which many families use for their wells.
